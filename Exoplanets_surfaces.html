Constraining the chemical and physical properties of rocky exoplanet surfaces

Recent observations from the Spitzer Space Telescope (LHS 3844 b) suggest that for some exoplanets, their rocky surfaces will dominate spectral observations. With the James Webb Space Telescope’s (JWST) launch, similar observations that span a broader range of infrared wavelengths and targets at higher precisions will become available. JWST will bring observations of high precision spectroscopic data at wavelengths up to 28.5 μm, opening up the possibility to access a broad range of exoplanetary systems. As seen in the figure above, Si-O vibrational features in the 7-13 μm and 15-25 μm thermal emission bands accessible with the JWST Mid-Infrared (MIRI) instrument will provide unprecedented information not only to conclusively identify an exoplanet as rocky but also reveal the composition of the planetary surface. Using a comprehesive spectrosopic library of rocks being generated by Dr. Emily First at Cornell University, Hapke modelling, and JWST noise modelling, I am creating a simulator for full-disk, low phase angle observations for rocky exoplanets. Future work will also involve the development of a Bayesian retrieval framework to determine our ability to distinguish between various compositional scenarios with observations from JWST and consider what capabilities of future missions/observatories would best to probe the geologic composition of exoplanets.
